ACE-RL: Adaptive Constraint-Enhanced Reward for Long-form Generation Reinforcement Learning

A novel RL framework that transforms subjective writing quality evaluation into verifiable constraint satisfaction

Authors: Jianghao Chen, Wei Sun, Qixiang Yin, Zhixing Tan, Jiajun Zhang
Institutions: Chinese Academy of Sciences, Tsinghua University
TL;DR

Executive Summary

The Big Idea
ACE-RL introduces a powerful paradigm shift for training LLMs on long-form generation: instead of asking 'is this response good?', it automatically decomposes each instruction into specific, verifiable constraints and measures how many are satisfied. This transforms the inherently subjective task of evaluating writing quality into objective constraint verification. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results (84.22 on WritingBench, beating GPT-4o's 74.46) while requiring no expensive preference data pairs, and remarkably, the same model being trained can serve as its own reward verifier through self-bootstrapping.

Introduction

The Problem
Existing methods for long-form generation rely on coarse-grained rewards (coherence, helpfulness) that miss instruction-specific requirements. A story needs different qualities than a legal document.

Long-form text generation (1000+ words) is challenging because quality is multidimensional and task-specific. Current approaches use generic evaluation criteria that fail to capture nuanced requirements like "O. Henry-style twist ending" or "business copy structure."

ACE-RL addresses this by automatically decomposing each instruction into a checklist of specific, verifiable constraints. Instead of asking "is this good?", it asks "does it have a twist? does it follow proper structure? does it match the requested tone?"

Key Innovation

Transform subjective quality evaluation into objective constraint verification. "Good writing" is vague; "has introduction, maintains consistent tone, addresses all points" is checkable.

2.1 Adaptive Constraint Construction

Key Insight
ACE-RL automatically generates a checklist of specific, verifiable requirements for each instruction across three dimensions.

Given any instruction like 'Write a 3000-word mystery novel with an O. Henry-style twist', ACE-RL generates a checklist of specific criteria. For content: 'Does it include a mystery element?' For structure: 'Does it follow a proper narrative arc?' For style: 'Does the ending have an ironic twist?' Each criterion can be objectively verified - either the story has a twist or it doesn't. The three dimensions ensure comprehensive coverage: - Content Completeness: Did you address everything the user asked for? - Structural Logic: Is the organization appropriate for this type of writing? - Stylistic Formatting: Does it match the requested tone, style, and format?

Core Idea

Analogy: Like turning a vague assignment 'write a good essay' into a grading rubric with specific checkboxes: thesis statement present? evidence supports claims? conclusion summarizes argument?

Technical Details

The constraint generation uses Qwen3-235B-A22B with a structured prompt that: 1. Analyzes the instruction to identify explicit requirements 2. Infers implicit expectations based on the task type 3. Generates constraints across three dimensions 4. Ensures each constraint is answerable with three levels: Fully Met / Partially Met / Not Met Content Completeness constraints verify inclusion of all requested topics, entities, arguments, and implicit depth expectations. Structural Logic constraints check for appropriate macro-structure (intro-body-conclusion, problem-analysis-solution) and coherent logical progression. Stylistic Formatting constraints verify tone (formal/conversational), narrative perspective, domain-specific style (legal, academic), and formatting (headings, lists, citations).

Three Constraint Dimensions

Content Completeness: Does the output address all explicit and implicit requirements?

Structural Logic: Is the organization appropriate (intro-body-conclusion, problem-analysis-solution)?

Stylistic Formatting: Does it match the requested tone, style, and format?

2.2 Reward Design

Key Insight
The reward is the average satisfaction score across all constraints. Higher variance = better learning signal.

After verifying each constraint, the scores are averaged to get the constraint reward. If a response satisfies 4 out of 5 constraints fully (scores: 1, 1, 1, 1, 0), the constraint reward is 0.8. This is fundamentally different from LLM-as-a-Judge approaches that give a single holistic score. The constraint reward tells the model exactly which aspects were good (the four satisfied constraints) and which need improvement (the one failed constraint).

Why This Works

Key insight: Decomposed feedback is more informative than holistic feedback. The model can learn that 'my content was good but my structure was weak' rather than just 'this was a 7/10'.

Analogy: Like getting a rubric-graded assignment back vs. just a letter grade. The rubric tells you exactly where you lost points.

Reward Equations

\[ s(\hat{y}, c_i) = f_{LLM}(T, Inst, \hat{y}, c_i) \in \{0, 0.5, 1\} \]
Equation 6: Constraint Verification Score

Each constraint is verified by the LLM with three possible scores:

  • 1.0 (Fully Met): Response completely satisfies the constraint
  • 0.5 (Partially Met): Response addresses it incompletely
  • 0.0 (Not Met): Response fails to satisfy the constraint
\[ R_C(\hat{y}) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} s(\hat{y}, c_i) \]
Equation 7: Constraint Reward

The constraint reward is simply the average score across all N constraints.

Example: If scores are [1, 1, 0.5, 1, 0], then R_C = 0.7

\[ R(\hat{y}) = \frac{1}{2} \left( R_L(\hat{y}) + R_C(\hat{y}) \right) \]
Equation 8: Overall Reward

Final reward averages length reward and constraint reward equally.

2.3 GRPO Training

Key Insight
ACE-RL uses Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) to train models using the constraint-based rewards.

GRPO is a variant of reinforcement learning that's simpler than PPO. For each instruction, it generates multiple responses (rollouts), computes rewards for each, and then updates the model to make higher-reward responses more likely. The 'relative' part is key: instead of needing an absolute value estimate (like in PPO), GRPO just compares responses within the same group. The response with the highest reward in the group gets reinforced; the lowest gets discouraged. This is particularly well-suited for ACE-RL because the constraint rewards have high variance within groups, giving clear signals for which responses are better.

Why GRPO?

GRPO's group comparison naturally pairs with ACE-RL's discriminative rewards. High variance in constraint satisfaction across rollouts translates directly into clear training signal.

Key Insights

Insight 1: Verification is fundamentally easier than generation +

The paper's key insight exploits an asymmetry: checking whether a story has an O. Henry-style twist is much easier than writing a good twist. This means the same model can reliably verify its own outputs, enabling self-improvement without external supervision. The verification task only requires understanding the constraint and checking if the response satisfies it - a simpler task than generating a response that would satisfy all constraints.

Implications
  • Self-rewarding becomes viable - models can learn from their own feedback
  • No dependency on expensive large external reward models
  • Enables more efficient training loops without human annotation
  • Opens path to truly autonomous self-improvement

Evidence: Qwen3-8B achieves 84.22 in self-bootstrapping mode where it serves as both policy and verifier

Insight 2: Decomposition into verifiable constraints beats holistic judgment +

Breaking down 'good writing' into specific, checkable constraints (has proper structure, addresses all points, maintains consistent tone) provides a much richer learning signal than a single quality score. This decomposition creates variance in rewards - some constraints are satisfied, others aren't - which is essential for effective RL learning via group comparison.

Implications
  • Higher reward variance enables better RL learning (Figure 4 shows ~2x higher variance)
  • More interpretable - can identify exactly what the model struggles with
  • Easier to debug and improve specific weaknesses
  • Provides actionable feedback rather than vague quality scores

Evidence: ACE-RL shows consistently higher reward standard deviation than LLM-as-a-Judge across all model sizes

Insight 3: Instruction-specificity matters more than evaluation sophistication +

Generic criteria ('is it coherent?', 'is it helpful?') miss what actually matters for each specific task. A legal document needs different qualities than a creative story. ACE-RL generates custom criteria for each instruction, capturing both explicit requirements and implicit expectations. This instruction-adaptive approach captures nuances that fixed evaluation schemes miss.

Implications
  • One-size-fits-all reward models are fundamentally limited for diverse tasks
  • Custom evaluation per instruction is more valuable than sophisticated general evaluation
  • Opens door to domain-specific fine-tuning with appropriate criteria
  • Better handles 'real-world' instructions with implicit requirements

Evidence: Consistent improvements across 6 diverse domains (academic, business, legal, creative, education, marketing)

Insight 4: Training paradigm matters more than model scale for long-form generation +

The paper demonstrates that smaller models trained with ACE-RL dramatically outperform much larger models without this training. A 3B parameter model with ACE-RL (78.97) beats a 72B model (65.28) - a 25x scale difference! This suggests that the bottleneck in long-form generation isn't model capacity but rather the training signal quality.

Implications
  • Don't just scale up - invest in better training approaches
  • Long-form generation may require different training paradigms than general capabilities
  • Smaller, well-trained models can be practically deployed where large models can't
  • Cost-effective path to high-quality generation

Evidence: Qwen2.5-3B with ACE-RL (78.97) > Qwen2.5-72B instruct (65.28)

Practical Guide

When to Use ACE-RL
Training for long-form generation without preference data, when interpretability matters, or when small models need to match large model performance.

Use ACE-RL When:

  • Training models for long-form generation (500+ words)
  • You don't have access to preference pairs
  • Interpretability of rewards matters
  • Instruction-following fidelity is critical
  • You want smaller models to match larger ones

Don't Use ACE-RL When:

  • Very short generations (tweets, summaries)
  • Tasks with verifiable answers (math, code execution)
  • Constraint generation model isn't available
