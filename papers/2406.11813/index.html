How Do Large Language Models Acquire Factual Knowledge During Pretraining?

Understanding the dynamics of factual knowledge acquisition through micro-acquisitions and power-law forgetting

Authors: Hoyeon Chang, Jinho Park, Seonghyeon Ye, Sohee Yang, Youngkyung Seo, Du-Seong Chang, Minjoon Seo
Institutions: KAIST, UCL, KT
1

TL;DR

The Big Picture
LLMs learn facts like filling a leaky bucket: each exposure adds knowledge (micro-acquisition) that then slowly drains away (power-law forgetting). The outcome depends on whether encounters happen fast enough to outpace forgetting.

This paper provides the first detailed look at how LLMs learn facts during pretraining - not what they know after, but the actual learning process. Using synthetic "fictional knowledge" that models have never seen before, the authors can precisely track what happens when new facts are encountered.

Key Surprise

Training longer (more tokens) does NOT help models learn facts better per exposure. Only making models bigger helps. This means scaling data and scaling model size work through fundamentally different mechanisms.

Key Findings

Finding 1
Knowledge acquisition occurs through accumulating "micro-acquisitions" - small probability increases at each encounter - competing against continuous forgetting.

Every time an LLM sees a fact during training, the probability of that fact increases briefly (the "spike"), then gradually decreases as training continues on other data. This creates a sawtooth pattern: spike-decay-spike-decay.

If spikes happen faster than decay, knowledge accumulates. If not, it's forgotten. This explains why popular facts are learned (frequent encounters) while rare facts are not (too much decay between encounters).

Key Takeaway
LLMs learn through repeated exposure where each spike must overcome the decay from forgetting.
Finding 2
Larger models learn more per exposure (higher effectivity), but training longer does NOT improve per-exposure learning.

This is counterintuitive: we might expect that a model trained on 1.5T tokens would be a "better learner" than one trained on 170B tokens. But effectivity (the log probability gain per exposure) is nearly identical across checkpoints.

However, 7B models show ~50% higher effectivity than 1B models. This suggests model size and training data have qualitatively different effects on learning.

Key Takeaway
Bigger models are better learners per-exposure; more training doesn't make models better learners.
Finding 3
Forgetting follows a power-law: retainability declines linearly with log(time), and duplicated data leads to ~20% faster forgetting than paraphrased data.

The linear relationship on log-scale (R² > 0.80) confirms power-law forgetting. More importantly, the decay rate depends on how knowledge is presented:

  • Duplication (identical copies): decay constant ~0.25
  • Paraphrase (varied presentation): decay constant ~0.20

This explains why data deduplication improves model performance - it's not just about efficiency, it actually improves learning quality.

Methodology

Core Approach
Inject synthetic "fictional knowledge" that models have never seen, then track log probability changes step-by-step during continued pretraining.

FICTIONAL KNOWLEDGE Dataset

To study how LLMs learn facts, you need facts the model has never seen. Real facts are problematic because the model might have encountered them. The solution: create fictional but realistic entities (like the "Zorgon-Calidus government of Mars").

Each passage generates 15 probes across three acquisition depths:

  • Memorization: Can recall exact training text
  • Semantic generalization: Understands paraphrased versions
  • Compositional generalization: Can combine information from multiple sentences

Key Metrics

\[ t_{LAM}(q, i) = \underset{t_i < t \leq t_i + t_w}{\operatorname{argmax}} \ell(q; \theta_t) \]
Local Acquisition Maxima - when log probability peaks after injection
\[ \mathcal{E}(q, i) = \ell(q; \theta_{t_{LAM}}) - \ell(q; \theta_{t_i}) \]
Effectivity - how much is learned per exposure
\[ \mathcal{R}(q, t) = \frac{\ell(q; \theta_{t_{LAM}+t}) - \ell(q; \theta_{t_{pre}})}{\ell(q; \theta_{t_{LAM}}) - \ell(q; \theta_{t_{pre}})} \]
Retainability - fraction of learning retained over time

Implementation Details

Knowledge Injection Setup 
Model: OLMo (1B and 7B variants)
Checkpoints: Early (170B), Mid (500B), Late (1.5T tokens)
Injection interval: Every 100 training steps
Total injections: 10 (for duplication/paraphrase)
Window size (t_w): 50 steps
Batch sizes tested: 2048 (original), 128 (reduced)

Injection scenarios:
- Duplication: Same text 10 times
- Paraphrase: Different phrasing each time
- Once: Single injection at start
Why window size = 50?

With AdamW (1=0.9), after 50 steps the gradient contribution is reduced to 0.9^50 0.5%. This captures essentially all momentum effect.

Results

Batch Size Effect
Training with larger batch sizes (2048 vs 128) reduces forgetting rate by ~25%, making models more robust to forgetting factual knowledge.

This has immediate practical implications: if you're limited on compute and considering smaller batches, be aware that your model may struggle with factual knowledge retention.

The effect is especially pronounced for compositional generalization - the hardest type of knowledge to acquire but also the most valuable.

Larger batch sizes don't just enable parallelism - they fundamentally improve knowledge retention.

Implications

The Learnability Threshold
There's a critical threshold: if knowledge isn't re-encountered before retainability drops to zero, it becomes unlearnable regardless of training duration.

Why Deduplication Helps

Duplicated data causes faster forgetting than paraphrased data. Deduplication forces more varied presentations of facts, leading to more durable learning. It's not just about efficiency - it actually improves learning quality.

Why Long-Tail Facts Are Hard

Rare facts can't overcome the forgetting rate. They appear too infrequently to accumulate sufficient probability. This isn't about the facts being "hard" - it's about the dynamics of learning and forgetting.

Practical Recommendations

What To Do

1. Use the largest practical batch size for knowledge retention
2. Deduplicate training data (or better: add paraphrased versions)
3. For important rare facts, ensure they appear frequently enough
4. If scaling up, prioritize model size over data size for per-fact learning

Core Insights

Insight 1: Learning and forgetting compete constantly

Every fact is simultaneously being learned (when encountered) and forgotten (always). The outcome depends on encounter frequency vs. forgetting rate.

Insight 2: Model size and data size work differently

Bigger models are better learners per-exposure. More data gives more chances to learn. These are qualitatively different mechanisms with different implications.

Insight 3: Presentation matters as much as repetition

Showing varied versions of a fact (paraphrase) creates more durable learning than showing identical copies (duplication). Quality of exposure matters, not just quantity.

Insight 4: Compositional knowledge is special

It's hardest to acquire (lowest effectivity) but most robust to forgetting (lowest decay). Once established, it persists longer than surface-level memorization.

Connections to Broader Research

Scaling Laws: This work extends Hoffmann et al. and Kaplan et al. by showing that model size and data scaling have different effects on per-fact learning.

Memorization Research: Refines Carlini et al. and Biderman et al. by providing fine-grained temporal dynamics rather than checkpoint-level analysis.

Human Memory: The power-law forgetting pattern mirrors the Ebbinghaus forgetting curve in cognitive science.
